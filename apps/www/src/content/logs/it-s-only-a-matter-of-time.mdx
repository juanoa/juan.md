---
title: "It's only a matter of time"
description: "How good and early decisions compound"
createdAt: 2026-04-13
---

I was born 27 years ago, and my life is far from being all that different from how I imagined it.

I was born into a middle-class family. We never went through hardship, but until I was 16, I only remember having been on a plane once in my life.

School bored me. I was always being told off because I spent class talking and distracting my classmates. I liked learning new things, especially in science subjects, but I picked things up very quickly and spent the rest of the time simply being bored.

For my parents, my getting good grades was important. Correction: very important. Although I liked learning new things, I hated memorizing. My mother spent hours and hours in my room making me repeat the material over and over again. I ended up hating studying, but I kept doing it because otherwise I knew there would be trouble at home.

Once I even forged a slip my teacher made us take to our parents so they could sign it and find out what grade we had gotten on a test. I got a 7. I drew an 8 over it. I was terrified of taking a low B home.

I don't remember school (or high school) as a good time in my life. Because of my father's job, we had to move when I was 8. I was very shy, I found it very hard to make friends, and soccer, which was the only thing boys at my new school were into, wasn't my thing.

Getting good grades didn't help me make more friends. Before long I was stuck with nicknames and labels that I had to carry until I finished high school. Some decided to bully me. I never liked violence. It always seemed completely unnecessary to me. I don't remember ever getting into a fight with anyone.

At 16 I changed high schools again and had decided that I wasn't going to go through everything I had gone through over those previous 10 years again. I made new friends. I got a girlfriend. But I kept studying hard. This time not so much because of pressure from my parents, but rather because university was close and I wanted good grades to study Computer Engineering.

>**🔙 Flashback**

I always knew what I wanted to study. I still have friends older than me who still don't know what they like in life. But as for me, my passion for computers came very early. When I was 6, the first computer came into our home. It was one of those old ones with a CRT screen. It had no Internet connection, I didn't have that until I was 14, but I didn't need it.

I spent hours and hours exploring settings, installing programs I had on floppy disks and deleting them again to start from scratch. That fascinated me. When we moved when I was 8, that computer was left behind and I didn't have another one until I turned 14.

My interest in technology grew. Back then, Tuenti was the thing. All my classmates had it. So did I, but it didn't interest me. I kept asking my mother for more and more technology, and as much as she could, she gave it to me. I loved being able to tinker with it.

At 16 I started writing on the Internet about technology, at 18 I discovered the world of SEO and started making some money ranking websites on Google and adding some advertising and affiliate links. I loved creating things, but SEO wasn't exactly the job of my dreams. Even so, the idea of being able to make my own money without having to depend on the weekly allowance my parents gave me fascinated me.

>**🔙 Flashback**

My mother was always very interested in me learning to read early. She always told the other mothers that I had been able to read an adapted version of Don Quixote when I was 5. That little book became my favorite book for a long time. I remember rereading it several times.

As a child I was an avid reader. I remember having a bookshelf in my room with quite a few books. But two things made me stop: the compulsory reading at school and how unpopular reading is at certain ages. If I was already afraid of being picked on for studying too much, I didn't even want to imagine what would happen if they also knew I liked reading.

I picked up this habit again when I started making some money with SEO in my teens. I remember the first book I read after that valley of almost 10 years without touching one: *In 100 Years We'll All Be Dead*, by Joan Boluda. It took me forever to finish it, and not because I didn't like it, quite the opposite, I loved it, but I couldn't read more than two pages without getting tired.

Entrepreneurship interested me for quite a long time. I remember the horrified look on my mother's face when I told her I wanted to start my own business and stop studying. Luckily that never happened, but I kept reading everything I found related to the topic. But something was no longer the same. I loved the first book I read, but I had a hard time digesting the rest. A bunch of disconnected ideas, with no apparent relationship whatsoever. Advanced books on fairly complex subjects like economics, psychology, or business.

I still remember the first time I picked up *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, by Kahneman. It was unreadable to me, but everyone spoke wonders of that book, so I had to read it. I put it down after a few chapters.

**Later I learned that books, like life, have an order, and that to get to point C, you first have to start at A and go through B. What mattered was not reading present-day gurus talking about things and concepts that already existed, but reading those who had invented them. It was about getting to know the foundations and the decisions they made in their minds to arrive at those conclusions, which they later captured in those books that are classics today.**

>A classic is something that cannot be done better

I finished Computer Engineering in 4 years. At last I was studying something I liked, but that idea faded when reality hit me hard. I found the degree quite difficult, especially the first 2 and a half years. I soon got discouraged when I saw that none of those algorithms, calculus classes, and physics labs looked anything like what I had in mind. A good friend once told me that her father, who had also studied Computer Engineering, used to remind her that the degree was not about learning useful things, but about proving that you have the tenacity and perseverance needed to finish it. I couldn't agree more.

Even so, I had a pretty good time at university. I made a lot of friends, partied quite a bit, and didn't get bad grades (even if it cost me blood, sweat, and tears). I met two classmates who were also into entrepreneurship, we got carried away and started an online store for stainless steel water bottles. We handled everything and learned a lot from it: buying the product from the supplier in China, managing the website and the online store, logistics, shipping, marketing, ...

A few months later we found out that economics professors were using us as an example in their entrepreneurship classes. It was fun, until it wasn't, and although years later I ended up at one of the biggest e-commerce companies in the world, selling bottles didn't feel like something I wanted to keep exploring.

All these projects that at first I didn't think would give me a great return and that I simply did because I felt like it soon produced their first result. When I finished university I didn't really know what to do. That was a small disappointment, because for as long as I can remember I had liked computers, and I didn't understand how I could be so lost at that moment. By chance I applied to Inditex's young talent program. They liked my story and accepted me. I didn't know it at the time, but I had just made a key decision in my life.

Whether the decision to leave my friends and family and move to the other side of the country was a good one was not something I could have guessed back then. **Humans are not only terrible at predicting the future, we also think we're pretty good at it. Throughout our lives we make decisions that compound over time. And when you stack good decisions on top of each other, the result ends up being exponentially positive.**

**At first it's hard to know which decision will turn out to be the right one. Many of them, the vast majority, won't be decisions as important as the one I made back then. Those big successful bets help, but they shouldn't be the core of your decisions. What I do know is that these good decisions reinforce each other so that you keep making good decisions. It's the compounding effect at its finest.**

My first two years at Inditex were as intense as they were exciting. I decided to learn a lot about frontend, about how things worked "under the hood," to study patterns, to see how others did things so I could try to apply it to the projects I was working on. I also learned the hard way how things work in places that big, the pros and cons of working in a corporation.

Two years later another new opportunity came my way. A friend, the same one I had started the bottle store with at university, shared a tweet with me from an account called *The Exponential Fellowship*. They were looking for young people eager to go build things in startups in Silicon Valley. The program's manifesto couldn't have resonated with me more. That program was for me. On top of that, at Inditex I was starting to run headfirst into certain things that didn't quite sit right with me. I applied. I interviewed with a startup. I almost had it. But I didn't make it through. The problem? English. The fellowship requirements clearly asked for a C1 level of English. I didn't know much about it, but I thought I could get up to speed in a few weeks. I underestimated the situation.

It was a hard blow. It would have been my second big bet that paid off, but in the end I stayed. Exponential opened me up to an enormous network of contacts that I kept exploring. I'm very grateful for all of that. But I decided to extend my stay at Inditex a couple more years. During that time I spent a few weeks helping Daniel Espejo with Omnia, but the change didn't fully convince me.

**Big bets usually have a problem: you have a lot to lose. But what happens when you have nothing to lose? Young people have a tremendous tactical advantage: we have nothing to lose. We don't have a mortgage to pay or children to support. When you have "nothing," you can either stay the same or come out ahead. So if you're going to bet, bet big. When we leave university, we have about 5 years of carte blanche. You can make decisions without being afraid of making a mistake. If you do make a mistake, many of them won't even have negative consequences. Never again in your life will you go through that window of opportunity. You have to make the most of it.**

After my failed opportunity to cross the pond, the next two years at Inditex were pretty monotonous. I kept having this idea in my head that this wasn't for me, but everyone told me it was the right thing. That it was a good job. That it was cold out there.

During my years at Inditex, I was lucky to cross paths with Nacho Gonzalez. Nacho came from Factorial, but had also been at Cabify and Spotahome. He told me how things worked in the startup world while I listened excitedly. In a way he helped me believe that outside there was something that interested me more. We talked outside of work, told each other about our side projects, discussed product. I think he could see that that wasn't my place.

A few years later, Danny Saltaren, a friend of Nacho's, decided to start MITO with Iñaki Berenguer and Arantxa Barcia. It had been a few months since Nacho and I no longer worked together, but he thought of me. He was still mulling it over, although I had already made up my mind. It was the perfect time to move. And unlike the rest of the startups I had been exploring over those previous months, I loved MITO's idea.

**If there is anything more important than doing things with passion, it's that others feel it too. People want to work with others who feel as strongly about ideas as they do.** Working with Nacho, having him see my interest in product, in solving real problems, in the startup world, led me to the second moonshot of my life. In October 2025, and without yet having a contract at MITO, I left Inditex. Unlike the doubts I had about moving to another city 4 years earlier, this time I was absolutely sure. I had to be part of this.

And now, 27 years after taking my first breath, everything is starting to make sense. The pieces of the puzzle are starting to fit together. I'm starting to feel an exponential curve rising beneath my feet. It's one of the best feelings I've had in my life.

It's now easier for me to make good decisions. Learning and reading no longer feels tedious once you have the foundations. I enjoy good physical and mental health. I have the best friends anyone could have. I'm in love with my mission in this world. I know all of this can collapse from one moment to the next, but all these decisions I have made, am making, and will make are turning me into a more resilient person, and, I hope, an antifragile one.

I don't have a plan for it, but I do have a system. **Systems are 10 times better than plans and 1000 times better than goals. When a system works well, you barely need to plan: many decisions become almost self-evident on their own, and the results of those decisions feed the system itself once again.**

That's where feedback loops come into play. They can drive you to grow exponentially or, on the contrary, trap you in a cycle that's hard to break. But little by little, by making good decisions and taking risks when you're young, the odds go up that everything will end up falling into place.

In the end, it was all just a matter of time.

I hope this story helps you.
