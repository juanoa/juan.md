I used to struggle with English. Not my cup of tea. I performed pretty well in school and in high school, but English was always my kryptonite.

I worked at Inditex for 4 years and I didn't need any language other than Spanish. That’s when I started consuming English content, but not too much. I used subtitles every single time; otherwise, I didn’t understand anything.

I changed my mindset a few years ago when I applied to [The Exponential Fellowship](https://www.goexponential.org) to get a visa to work in Silicon Valley. I read the manifesto and skipped the part about having a "good English level." I thought language wouldn’t be a real problem and that I’d learn it fast. I was close to being selected by a startup, but when they tried to interview me in English, it was impossible. I was automatically rejected, obviously.

Then I understood the importance of English, especially in tech, so I took action. I started attending conversational classes, consuming more English content, and watching films and TV series without subtitles. It was pretty hard. I'm a learner. I always want to learn new things, but languages aren’t easy for me.

Fast forward to today, my English level is better. I'm writing this post with translation tools or ChatGPT. I'm proud of myself. I feel better learning English. Now it makes sense to me. I'm not learning English for the sake of it. I'm learning English because when you learn this language, thousands of doors instantly open for you: new jobs, new media, new content, new interesting people you can follow.

## Why collocations

As I mentioned, I tried a lot of things to improve my English. There are a lot of apps out there for most of those things. However, I was missing something.

A collocation is a group of words that are commonly used together. For example:

- Leave a comment
- Ask for feedback

Keeping those collocations in mind helped me a lot in order to compose better and faster sentences. I have been collecting a lot of them over the past few months and now I’ve published them all at [collocations.juan.md](collocations.juan.md).

## Building the site

Let’s start with how I built the site. As you can see, it’s a simple website that I developed using Astro.

It contains 3 main kinds of pages:

- Home
- Collocation page
- Newsletter (I’ll cover this later in the article)

The homepage is a basic page with a **collocations searcher** (that appears on almost all pages) and a grid with the last 9 collocations. My idea is to keep adding new collocations to this repository over time.

### Style and design

I used the same UI as `www.juan.md`. That was the main reason behind migrating to a monorepo. Basically, my whole design system is based on **Tailwind** and **Shadcn**, with a custom theme I generated using the [Shadcn creator mode](https://ui.shadcn.com/create).

### Content

My first idea was to save the collocations data in a JSON file in the repository, but this wouldn’t be compatible with the future newsletter.

So I started a project on [Supabase](https://supabase.com/) and created a `collocations` database. I maintain a JSON file with some collocation examples for DEV mode, but **the production build fetches collocations from Supabase**.

It would be great to automatically roll out the app every time I add a new collocation to the table, maybe using a webhook. _Adding it to my to-do list..._

### Random

I thought that adding a "Random" button could be a good idea. **I was inspired by the _"I'm feeling lucky"_ Google button**.

The idea is to discover new collocations instead of searching for one you already have in mind. Once you start a "random session," you will see the random button on each page, so you can go down your own rabbit hole 🐇.

## Building the newsletter

The second important feature of this little project is the [newsletter](https://collocations.juan.md/newsletter). The idea is to send a daily email with an English collocation and examples of how to use it at work.

### Collecting the emails

To collect emails, I added a small form with an email field and a job position selector. The idea is to generate custom examples according to your role: SWE, product manager, product designer, or C-level.

This form is protected by [Cloudflare Turnstile](https://www.cloudflare.com/es-es/application-services/products/turnstile/), a free alternative to Google reCAPTCHA.

The emails are saved in Resend, and I divide the audience by topics. Each job position (SWE, PM, PD, C-level) is a different topic in Resend.

### Email template

To keep the same style in every email, I created an email template using the Templates feature in Resend.

![Resend template used for the collocations newsletter](/content/logs/how-i-built-collocations/resend-template.webp)

### Generating the emails

The newsletter is 100% AI-generated. Every weekday at 18:00, a **Supabase Edge Function** runs with the following instructions:

1. **Get an unused collocation** (this is important to avoid sending duplicate emails over time).
2. Generate the content for the Resend template variables using the **Vercel AI SDK** and GPT 5.4.
3. Send me an email with the **draft**.
4. **Schedule** the email for each topic for the next day at 10 AM.
5. **Persist the used collocation** in the Supabase DB to avoid it being used again.
